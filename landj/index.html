Seattle

Seattle, on Puget Sound in the Pacific Northwest, is surrounded by water, mountains and evergreen forests, and encompasses thousands of acres of parkland (hence its nickname, "Emerald City"). Home to Microsoft and Amazon.com. The futuristic Space Needle, a legacy of the 1962 World’s Fair, is its most recognizable landmark. For more see the Visit Seattle web site.

Celebrity Cruise

The Celebrity Solstice, namesake and first of the Solstice-Class ships, innovated cruising for Celebrity.

Learn more about the ship

This ship was the first to introduce the Lawn Club a spacious area on the ship with fresh and beautiful green grass, perfect for picnics or a game of catch. This was also the first ship to have The Hot Glass Show where passengers watch and learn as glass blowers talk about this beautiful art form and create beautiful glass objects in front of their eyes.

For dinner, the Solstice’s main dining room features a two-story wine tower and is one of the few ships with the Silk Harvest Restaurant, where you can indulge in dishes like Green Curry Stir Fried Pork and Shrimp & Scallop Shumai.

For more info about the cruise, see CruiseWeb.

Ketchikan

Ketchikan is known as Alaska’s “first city” due to its location at the southern tip of the Inside Passage.

Learn more about Ketchikan

It is the first city you reach as you cruise north, and for many visitors, their first introduction to the beauty and majesty of Alaska.

Founded as a salmon cannery site in 1885, Ketchikan's livelihood was initially fishing and for years the city was known as the “Canned Salmon Capital of the World.” Logging became an important industry as well, and when cruise ships started plying the waters of the Inside Passage, Ketchikan naturally became a popular port of call.

See the TravelAlaska.com Ketchikan page for more information.

Juneau

Downtown Juneau sits snugly between Mount Juneau, Mount Roberts and Gastineau Channel, and is a maze of narrow streets running past a mix of new structures, old storefronts and quaint houses featuring early 19th century architecture left over from the town’s early gold mining days.

Learn more about Juneau

Just a short drive from downtown, one of the city’s most iconic sites sits at the edge of a residential neighborhood: Mendenhall Glacier. The glacier sits within the Tongass National Forest – the largest national forest in the United States – and visitors to the area can watch icebergs calve off its frozen face into Mendenhall Lake, go for a hike or check out the Mendenhall Glacier Visitor Center.

Skagway

If you were going to visit just one town in Southeast Alaska, Skagway would be a top contender. While other towns in the region make for great jumping-off spots, this tiny community is a destination unto itself.

Learn more about Skagway

Part of Skagway’s charm lies in its preservation: the Park Service has rescued some 15 buildings from the Gold Rush era, and today those historic relics make this small town—just 23 blocks long and four blocks wide—as authentic an example of 19th-century life as you’ll find anywhere.

See Alaska.org for more on Skagway.

Learn more about Victoria

In Victoria, heritage architecture, colourful gardens and traditions like afternoon tea mix with outdoor adventure, authentic culinary experiences and enviable cocktail and craft beer scenes.
